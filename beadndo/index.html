Waste-to-Energy and Sustainability in Europe

A compact visual study of how European countries generate waste, distribute it across treatment pathways, and connect these choices to energy transition and sustainability performance.

Waste management is not only an environmental question. It also reflects how societies consume, how infrastructure is organized, and how policy systems balance recycling, landfill reduction, energy recovery, and climate goals. This project uses public European indicators to explore that relationship through an EU-wide, guided data story with particular attention to Romania and Hungary in their wider regional context.

The website is built as a lightweight GitHub Pages project using HTML, CSS, JavaScript, and Vega-Lite. All figures rely on cleaned local extracts from official public data sources, primarily Eurostat and the World Bank.

Project Scope

How to read this project

Research question

The central question is how waste generation and treatment vary across Europe, and whether stronger recycling and recovery systems are associated with better performance on wider sustainability indicators.

Story structure

The page moves from waste pressure, to treatment choices, to wider sustainability context. This sequence is intended to make the figures read as an argument rather than as isolated charts.

Data integration

The data/ folder already mirrors the intended workflow: the charts use cleaned local files derived from official public datasets, with the broadest EU-country coverage available for each indicator and transparent source notes in DATA_SOURCES.md.

Main Findings

What the current evidence suggests

Waste generation remains uneven

EU-wide coverage shows that municipal waste generation varies sharply across member states, suggesting that consumption patterns, service systems, and reporting structures still differ substantially.

Low landfill often coincides with stronger system capacity

Countries with minimal landfill tend to rely on a combination of recycling and energy recovery rather than a single dominant pathway.

Recycling and circularity are related but not identical

Strong municipal recycling does not automatically imply the highest circular material use, which depends on the broader economy as well as waste policy.

Romania and Hungary illustrate two different transition positions

Romania remains far more landfill-reliant than Hungary, while Hungary posts stronger recycling, circularity, and energy recovery values. Both therefore help anchor the broader EU comparison in Central and Eastern Europe.

Project Purpose

Why this topic matters

Waste policy sits at the intersection of environmental protection, resource management, and energy strategy. This website is designed as a compact course-project portfolio that introduces key indicators related to waste generation, treatment pathways, renewable energy, circularity, and sustainability performance in Europe.

The aim is to combine a clear visual structure with transparent use of public data, creating a compact portfolio that can support a concise academic data story without relying on unnecessary technical complexity.

Analytical Lens

The project does not assume that waste-to-energy is inherently positive or negative. Instead, it examines how energy recovery fits within wider patterns of landfill reduction, recycling performance, and sustainability transition.

Part I

Waste pressure and treatment choices

The first step is to establish how much municipal waste is generated, then examine what happens to that waste after collection. Together, these figures frame the structural pressure placed on national waste systems.

Figure 1

How much municipal waste is generated per person?

This figure compares municipal waste generated per person across EU countries in 2023. The default view shows the full ranking with equal visual treatment across countries, while the country filter can be used to isolate any subset for closer comparison. The figure offers a first view of where municipal systems face relatively high or low pressure from household and household-like waste streams.

Figure 2

Where does municipal waste go after collection?

This figure compares three key treatment pathways in 2023: recycling, landfill, and incineration with energy recovery. The shares are calculated relative to municipal waste generated, which helps illustrate how national systems distribute waste across contrasting policy models. The default view keeps all available countries in the comparison, and the filters allow the reader to narrow both the country set and the treatment pathways shown. Latvia is omitted here because the full 2023 treatment breakdown needed for this comparison is not available in the harmonized source.

Interim Takeaway

The treatment figures suggest that lower landfill reliance is often associated with more developed waste-management systems, but the balance between recycling and energy recovery still varies sharply across countries.

Part II

Circularity and the wider transition

The next pair of figures moves beyond waste operations alone. It asks whether countries that appear stronger on waste management also look stronger on energy transition and circular-economy indicators.

Figure 3

How quickly is renewable energy expanding?

This figure places waste-to-energy within a wider energy-transition context. The default view includes all available EU countries, and the filters can be used to reduce visual density when a smaller comparison is more useful. The optional EU average benchmark helps place any selected country set inside the broader transition trend.

Figure 4

Do higher recycling rates imply stronger circularity?

This figure compares the municipal recycling rate with the circular material use rate for the latest common year available across the EU countries. It is useful for assessing whether stronger recycling outcomes are accompanied by broader progress toward a circular economy. The full scatter shows the overall EU spread by default, while the country filter can be used to focus the comparison on a smaller group.

Germany remains one of the strongest recycling performers in the dataset and illustrates how landfill can be reduced through a more diversified mix of recovery pathways.

Sweden stands out less for waste generation than for how strongly energy transition and energy recovery are integrated into a broader low-landfill model.

Part III

Climate context and energy recovery

The final section places waste systems in a broader emissions context and then returns directly to the waste-to-energy question through an interactive comparison of energy recovery trends.

Figure 5

How different is the climate context across countries?

This figure adds environmental context through per-capita greenhouse gas emissions. Although the indicator reflects many parts of the economy rather than waste management alone, it helps place national waste and energy patterns within a wider climate-policy context and reminds the reader that waste policy sits inside a larger emissions system. The default ranking shows the full EU pattern, while the country filter allows narrower comparisons when needed.

Figure 6

How has energy recovery from waste changed over time?

This interactive figure tracks municipal waste sent to energy recovery over time. It serves as a practical waste-to-energy proxy based on harmonized Eurostat data. The default view includes all available countries, and the filters can be used to compare any smaller set with or without the EU average benchmark. Its purpose is not to prove causal effects, but to show how differently this pathway is used across national waste systems.

Methods

Data scope, sources, and limitations

Data sources

The project uses cleaned extracts from Eurostat and the World Bank. Where the source supports it, the site uses full EU-member coverage rather than a small country sample. Source links and file mappings are documented in DATA_SOURCES.md.

Comparability

The figures are designed for cross-country comparison, but differences in reporting practice, system boundaries, and national infrastructure still matter when interpreting results. One practical example is Figure 2, where Latvia is excluded because the harmonized 2023 treatment breakdown is incomplete for the required operations.

Interpretive caution

The charts are descriptive rather than causal. They are intended to identify patterns, contrasts, and useful questions for further analysis rather than definitive policy rankings.

Conclusion

What this portfolio ultimately shows

Taken together, the figures suggest that municipal waste management in Europe is best understood as part of a broader sustainability system rather than as a standalone technical sector. Landfill reduction, recycling strength, energy recovery, renewable transition, and climate context do not move in perfect lockstep, but they are clearly connected.

That makes waste-to-energy an analytically useful but incomplete lens: it matters most when read alongside wider treatment choices and broader transition indicators. Romania and Hungary illustrate this especially clearly: both sit inside the same EU policy space, yet their treatment mixes and wider sustainability positions still differ meaningfully. As a result, the project aims to show not only what the data say, but how public indicators can be organized into a coherent, EU-wide comparative story.
